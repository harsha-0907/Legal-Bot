## Summary of the Judgment in Madan Lal & Ors. Etc. v. State of Punjab Etc. 

**1. Constitutional References:**

* None explicitly stated. However, the judgment involves the interpretation and application of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894, which is a piece of legislation passed by Parliament under the authority of the Constitution of India. 

**2. Key Legal Questions:**

* Whether the High Court was justified in applying a 25% cut to the base value of land acquired by the State on the ground that Punjab was under the "shadow of terrorism" during the relevant period. 
* Whether the High Court's method of calculating the base value of the land, including the application of a yearly appreciation rate of 7.5%, was correct.

**3. Legal Doctrines:**

* **Land Acquisition:** The case involves the interpretation and application of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894, particularly the process for determining compensation for acquired land.
* **Valuation of Property:** The court considered the principles of valuing property for acquisition purposes, including the consideration of market value, appreciation, and development cuts.

**4. Outcome:**

* The Supreme Court allowed the appeals and set aside the High Court's judgment in part. 
* The court upheld the High Court's calculation of the base value of the land but removed the 25% cut attributed to the "shadow of terrorism."

**5. Reliefs or Orders:**

* The High Court's 25% deduction on account of terrorism was removed. 
* The appellants were entitled to compensation based on a base value of Rs. 92.56 per square yard, subject to a 25% development cut.
* The net value was determined to be Rs. 69.42 per square yard.
* The State of Punjab was ordered to pay the difference in compensation to the appellants within eight weeks. 

**6. Reasoning:**

* The Court found that the High Court's decision to apply a further cut to the base value on account of terrorism was unjustified, as there was no evidence to support the claim that transactions were taking place at depressed prices due to the security situation.
* The Court held that while the "shadow of terrorism" might impact the market value of land, there was no basis for assuming a 25% reduction in the base value.
* The Court acknowledged the High Court's reliance on the Oil and Natural Gas Corporation (ONGC) case (2008) for calculating the base value, but maintained that the 7.5% yearly appreciation was a factor to be considered in determining the base value and not a fixed percentage.

**7. Statutory Interpretation:**

* The Court interpreted the Land Acquisition Act, 1894, primarily concerning the provisions related to determining compensation for acquired land.
* The Court examined the process of valuing property for acquisition, highlighting the role of market value, appreciation, and development cuts.

**8. Precedents Cited:**

* General Manager, Oil and Natural Gas Corporation Limited v. Rameshbhai Jivanbhai Patel & Another (2008) 14 SCC 745. This case was cited for its use of a 7.5% yearly appreciation rate in determining land value, but the Supreme Court clarified that it is not a fixed percentage.

**Conclusion:**

The Supreme Court's decision underscores the need for a careful and nuanced approach to determining compensation for acquired land.  The Court highlighted the importance of considering specific evidence and circumstances rather than applying fixed percentages or making assumptions when assessing the value of land. The judgment also emphasizes the importance of  a fair and equitable process in land acquisition proceedings.
