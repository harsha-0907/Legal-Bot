## Summary of the Judgment in L & T Finance Ltd. v. Pramod Kumar Rana & Anr.

**1. Constitutional References:** 

* None explicitly stated.

**2. Key Legal Questions:**

* Whether the issuance of bailable warrants against a company director was warranted when the company was represented by its counsel and authorized representatives.
* Whether the allegations of coercion and threats made by the complainant against the company representatives justified the issuance of warrants at this stage of the proceedings.

**3. Legal Doctrines:**

* Principles of fair trial and due process.
* Right to be heard and representation.
* Interference with administration of justice.
* Issuance of warrants as a last resort.

**4. Outcome:**

* The Supreme Court allowed the appeals filed by the original opposite party No. 2 (L&T Finance Ltd.) and quashed the orders of the National Commission dated 26.08.2021, 03.09.2021, and 16.09.2021.
* The issuance of bailable warrants against the company director (Shri Dinanath Mohandas Dubhashi) was set aside.

**5. Reliefs or Orders:**

* Quashing of the National Commission's orders regarding the personal appearance of the company director and the issuance of bailable warrants.
* Permission for the company to be represented by its authorized representatives and counsel.

**6. Reasoning:**

* The Court acknowledged the seriousness of the allegations made by the complainant, but emphasized that these allegations were yet to be proven.
* The Court found that the company was adequately represented by its counsel and authorized representatives, and the Director's personal presence was not crucial at this stage.
* The Court held that bailable warrants should be issued as a last resort, only in cases where a party is not cooperating or refusing to appear despite representation.
* The Court emphasized the importance of allowing both parties a fair opportunity to present their case and cautioned against interfering with the administration of justice prematurely.

**7. Statutory Interpretation:**

* None explicitly stated.

**8. Precedents Cited:**

* None explicitly stated.

**9. Legal Arguments Accepted/Rejected:**

* The appellant argued that the issuance of warrants was unwarranted considering the company's representation. This argument was accepted by the Court.
* The appellant argued that the allegations against the company director were unsubstantiated and did not justify the issuance of warrants. This argument was implicitly accepted by the Court.
* The respondent did not appear and therefore did not present any arguments.

**Conclusion:** 

The Supreme Court's decision highlights the importance of ensuring fair process and due representation for all parties involved in legal proceedings. The Court emphasized that while serious allegations deserve due consideration, they should not be used to unfairly target individuals or disrupt the normal course of legal proceedings.
